Non-communicable diseases reaching epidemic proportions

The movement of people from the countryside into cities has had a drastic effect on lifestyles in China, as these become more sedentary and calorific intake has increased as incomes have grown. The Chinese population is also more exposed to alcohol and smoking, as well as pollution. Such factors are producing a growth in non-communicable diseases that has reached epidemic proportions.


China has overtaken the United States in terms of diabetes prevalence, with approximately 11.6% of adults having diabetes, according to a 2013 study by The Journal of the American Medical Association. This compares with a prevalence rate of 11.3% in the United States. In absolute terms, the study's finding that 114 million people suffer from the disease was an upward revision of about 21.6 million from prior estimates. That study found that only 25.8% of people with diabetes in China were receiving treatment, and of those only 39.7% achieved adequate glycaemic control. In addition, 50% of adults are estimated to have pre-diabetes, putting them at risk for cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, and cancer. The International Diabetes Federation estimated that if all eligible patients were to receive treatment, expenditure on treating diabetes patients could grow by 29% to CNY22 billion (USD3.4 billion).

Cancer is now the biggest killer in China, according to the World Health Organization (WHO), and home to one in four cancer deaths worldwide. Approximately 2.2 million people in China die from cancer every year, with the lowest recovery rates primarily in lung cancer, liver cancer, and breast cancer (source: WHO, 2015). There is a notably greater prevalence of lung cancers among the urban populace, while liver cancer is most prevalent cancer in rural areas.

Infectious diseases

Although chronic diseases remain the primary political concern, China has by no means rid itself of infectious diseases. In 2013, the most widespread infectious disease was viral hepatitis, with 1.25 million new cases and 739 deaths (Chinese ministry of health, 2013). Other widespread infectious diseases included pulmonary tuberculosis, dysentery, syphilis, and gonorrhoea.

Pneumonic plague, categorised as a Class A infectious disease in China, has been all but eradicated. Nevertheless, sporadic outbreaks still took place during the last decade – including in Qinghai, which saw the latest outbreak claim three lives, with nine other confirmed cases as of 3 August 2009. The local government adopted strict quarantine measures by closing off the original outbreak area, the Ziketan Town, which has some 10,000 residents.

At the beginning of 2015, China had over 90,000 reported cases of hand-foot-mouth disease (HFMD) – on a par with previous years – including 10 HFMD fatalities (source: WHO Western Pacific Region, 2015).

Primary care

Although China's healthcare provision remains dominated by hospitals, the government has made developing the primary-health system one of the cornerstones of future reforms. Patients in China have traditionally preferred to visit hospitals, even when suffering from only minor ailments, a mindset developed following the collapse of the Mao-era "barefoot doctors" system – the commune-based Co-operative Medical Scheme (CMS) – during the 1980s. CMS membership fell from 90% in 1979 to 11% in 1983, meaning that many patients preferred to bypass local levels of health provision in favour of the nearest county or city-level hospital. This has also helped contribute to hospitals' dominance in terms of being the location for patients to purchase their prescription drugs.
